## What to Look for in Nursery Night Lighting

### Color Temperature Matters Most

The single most important feature is color temperature, measured in Kelvin (K). For night feeding, stay between 2000K and 2700K — this warm, amber light won't suppress melatonin production like cooler blue light does. I made the mistake early on of using a "soft white" 3000K bulb, thinking it was warm enough. The difference when I switched to 2700K was immediately noticeable in how quickly we both fell back asleep.

### Dimming Capability is Essential

Fixed-brightness lights are rarely the right brightness for every situation. Some nights you need just a hint of light to see the baby's face, while other times you might need to check for spit-up or change a diaper. Look for lights with at least 3 brightness levels, though continuous dimming is even better.

### Safety Considerations

Place lights where curious toddlers can't reach them once your baby becomes mobile. Avoid lights that get hot — LED options stay cool to the touch. For plug-in models, ensure cords are secured and outlets have safety covers.

### Power Source Flexibility

I strongly recommend having both plug-in and battery/rechargeable options available. Plug-in lights provide reliability for permanent installations, while portable lights give you flexibility to bring light exactly where you need it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How bright should night feeding lights be?

The ideal brightness is just enough to see clearly without causing you to squint or feel wide awake. In technical terms, this is typically 1-5 lumens for ambient lighting, though it depends on room size and personal preference. Start dim and increase as needed.

### Will night lights keep my baby awake?

Warm-toned lights (2700K or lower) shouldn't interfere with sleep when kept dim. In fact, many babies find consistent low-level lighting more comforting than complete darkness. Avoid blue or white light, which can be stimulating.

### Are red lights really better for night vision?

Yes, red light preserves your natural night vision better than any other color. This means when you turn off the light, your eyes adjust back to darkness more quickly. However, red lights can make it harder to assess your baby's color/health, so they're not ideal for everyone.

### How long do LED night lights typically last?

Quality LED night lights typically last 25,000-50,000 hours of use. For a light used 2-3 hours nightly, that's roughly 20-40 years of service life. The main failure points are usually the power supplies or control circuits, not the LEDs themselves.

### Should I use multiple lights or one central light?

I recommend multiple small lights rather than one bright central light. This creates more even lighting without harsh shadows and gives you backup if one fails. A combination of a plug-in light near the feeding area and a motion-activated strip along walking paths works particularly well.

## Creating the Perfect Night Feeding Environment

Beyond just the light itself, consider the overall environment. Position lights to avoid creating shadows where you need to see most clearly — typically around the baby's face and your hands. If breastfeeding, a small light positioned behind your shoulder provides excellent illumination without shining in the baby's eyes.

For bottle feeding, I've found that a dimmer light works better than brighter illumination. You need to see the bottle and baby clearly, but not so much light that it becomes stimulating. The rechargeable touch lamps work particularly well here because you can adjust brightness with one hand while holding the baby.

Consider your path from bedroom to nursery as well. Motion-activated strip lights or small plug-in units create a "landing strip" effect that prevents stumbling in the dark. I learned this lesson after bumping into furniture one too many times during those bleary-eyed middle-of-the-night walks.
