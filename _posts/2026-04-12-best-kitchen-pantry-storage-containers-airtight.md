## Maintenance and Care Tips

### Cleaning and Sanitizing

Wash new containers thoroughly before first use to remove manufacturing residues. Most quality containers are dishwasher-safe, but hand washing extends seal life. Pay special attention to lid gaskets – food particles trapped here compromise seal effectiveness.

For deep cleaning, soak containers in warm water with baking soda to eliminate odors. Avoid abrasive cleaners that might scratch clear surfaces or damage seals.

### Maximizing Seal Life

Store containers with lids slightly ajar when not in use – this prevents gasket compression and extends seal life. Replace containers if seals become cracked or lose elasticity.

Keep locking mechanisms clean and dry. Food particles in hinges or clips can prevent proper sealing and make operation difficult.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long do airtight containers keep food fresh?

Quality airtight containers extend freshness significantly compared to original packaging. Properly stored flour stays fresh for 6-8 months versus 3-4 months in paper bags. Cereal maintains crispness for months instead of going stale within weeks. Rice, pasta, and dried beans can maintain quality for over a year when stored properly.

### Can airtight containers prevent pantry pests?

Yes, truly airtight containers create an effective barrier against pantry moths, weevils, and other common pests. The key is ensuring complete airtightness – even small gaps allow pest entry. Transfer foods immediately after purchase to prevent introducing existing pest eggs into clean containers.

### What's the difference between "airtight" and "watertight"?

Airtight containers prevent air exchange but may not be completely watertight. Watertight containers seal against liquids but also provide airtight storage. For pantry storage, airtight sealing is sufficient for maintaining freshness and preventing pests.

### Should I transfer all pantry items to containers?

Focus on items most susceptible to staleness, pests, or moisture: flour, sugar, cereal, rice, pasta, nuts, and baking ingredients. Items with excellent original packaging (canned goods, sealed jars) don't require transfer. Consider your usage patterns – frequently used items benefit most from easy-access containers.

### How do I test if a container is truly airtight?

Fill the container with water, seal completely, and invert for several minutes. True airtight seals won't leak. For a more sensitive test, place a piece of paper towel inside, seal the container, and submerge in water. If the towel stays dry, the seal is airtight.
